I’m one of the people who are eagerly waiting for summer to come. Why, especially when I live in the tropical rain-forest area that I can feel the summer the whole year, you ask? Well I have my own reason. The reason is summer night sky.

I started my own long session of stargazing around early January this year, during the break after went through five semester of my degree. But I stopped right after summer end to continue my final semester. Being a good chap, I’m getting accepted into local university to complete my internship. After settling in for two months I finally decided to start my stargazing session again with similar season of the sky.

Living in the equator and tropical rain-forest region, not everyday you are blessed with clear skies. Clouds are to be expected, usually almost every night. Humidity in this area is so high sometimes you don’t feel cold at all during night time that you don’t really have to cover yourself with thick clothes. The good part, however, is you can see almost all 88 constellations which otherwise impossible to see in either northern hemisphere or southern hemisphere.

What’s so interesting about summer (or winter, let’s call it summer for the sake of simplicity) night sky is you can see a lot of recognizable constellations and bright stars as well as deep sky objects such as nebulae and star clusters. Ask any casual sky gazers whether they can identify or at least notice this star pattern during summer night.

{% include image.html url="/assets/images/waiting-summer/1.png" description="Late November around 10 PM, the constellation started to rise from the horizon. (Source: Stellarium)" %}

Chance are, they get the name correct if they remember what they have learned in school before. Orion the Hunter is easily the most recognizable and noticeable constellation among other constellations out there. During summer, you will see this beautiful constellation as early as 7 pm depending on the night [as the stars rise 4 minutes earlier each day](http://oneminuteastronomer.com/9911/how-the-sky-moves/). For instance, at 8 pm Orion is seen to rise just above the horizon during mid-December but it already reached the [zenith](http://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/zenith) during late February at the same time. From this constellation alone you can see two bright stars, namely Betelgeuse and Rigel. At the center, you can see the straight line called Orion Belt. Below the belt you will notice a curved patchy line as the Hunter hanging his sword there. Grab a binocular (or telescope, or stand in a dark enough place without any light pollution) and try to focus at the center of Orion’s Sword. What you see depending on the equipment is something like this.

{% include image.html url="/assets/images/waiting-summer/2.jpeg" description="Orion Nebula (M42) in the constellation Orion (Source: [Eric Teske astronomy blog](https://www.ericteske.com/2014/02/complete-ias-noviceurban-observing-list.html))" %}

This patch is called Orion Nebula. Orion Nebula is one of the brightest [nebula](http://coolcosmos.ipac.caltech.edu/ask/224-What-is-a-nebula-) on the sky, therefore we can see it with our naked eyes providing the place is dark enough and our eyes are adapted enough to the dark. For casual stargazers, this is one of the easy target and a must-see deep-sky object. This explains why Orion Nebula is a frequent target for astrophotography aside of other visible objects such as planets. Aside of Orion Nebula, there is another famous nebula in the constellation Orion. Located just south of the star Alnitak, this nebula resembles horse’s head and therefore its name is called [Horsehead Nebula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Horsehead_Nebula).

We are just talking about one constellation in the summer sky. Orion can be used to hop to another constellations and asterisms as well, make it very useful especially for budding stargazers to learn and appreciate the diversity beneath the heaven. For instance, you can follow the line of the Belt of Orion from left to right until you found another bright star called Aldebaran. Aldebaran is part of constellation Taurus the Bull. From there, you can continue following the line until you see a small cluster.

{% include image.html url="/assets/images/waiting-summer/3.png" description="Visual guide on how to find the Pleiades (M45) by star-hopping" %}

What’s this blueish star cluster? It’s called the Pleiades, or by popular name, the Seven Sisters. Pleiades is an [open cluster](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Open_cluster) located in the constellation Taurus and consists of bright blue, hot stars formed hundred million years ago. Like Orion Nebula, Pleiades also visible to naked eyes and can be recognized pretty easily by anyone. So far we have found two out of 110 [Messier objects](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Messier_object), Orion Nebula (M43) and the Pleiades (M45) guided by just a constellation. As you progress you will find yourself trying to discover all of the objects in Messier catalog, which is pretty neat stuff to do.

There are many more that summer sky has to offer. You can find another bright stars such as Sirius, Capella, Procyon, and Pollux. You can even combine them to make another asterism called [Winter Hexagon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Winter_Hexagon) (also called Summer Hexagon for southern counterpart) consists of 6 to 7 bright stars. Most bright stars is an alpha to their own constellations, so try to figure out which bright star is belong to which constellation. If you like to dig deeper, try to find deep-sky objects such as Crab Nebula, M35 open cluster, Beehive cluster and some other objects although you might need to consider buying optical aid for better experience.

That’s it for today. I wish you all clear skies and truthfully I did not notice my supposedly quick rant session about how I want to stargaze this summer turns into short introductory to stargazing. Hope you enjoy looking towards the heaven. Have fun.
